Information about the Lakers

The Los Angeles Lakers are an American professional basketball team based in Los Angeles. The Lakers compete in the National Basketball Association (NBA) as a member of the league's Western Conference Pacific Division. The Lakers play their home games at Crypto.com Arena, an arena shared with the NBA's Los Angeles Clippers. The Lakers are one of the most successful teams in the history of the NBA, and have won 17 NBA championships, tied with the Boston Celtics for the most in NBA history.

The franchise began with the 1947 purchase of a disbanded team, the Detroit Gems of the National Basketball League (NBL). The new team began playing in Minneapolis, Minnesota, calling themselves the Minneapolis Lakers. Initially a member of the NBL, the Lakers won the 1948 NBL championship before joining the rival Basketball Association of America, where they would win five of the next six championships, led by star George Mikan. After struggling financially in the late 1950s following Mikan's retirement, they relocated to Los Angeles before the 1960-61 season.

Most Influential Lakers Owners

Bob Short

Bob Short acquired the Minneapolis Lakers in 1957 and eventually moved their team to the more affordable Los Angeles. Although the owner of the team, Short acted as general manager and was responsible for bringing basketball great, Elgin Baylor to the roster. Short was also influential in working with Mohs in bringing possibly the most iconic basketball figure to the NBA: its logo, Jerry West. When the Lakers drafted West, Short helped reunite him with his former head coach Fred Schaus from West Virginia. The two would become some of the most influential Lakers figures of all time. As an owner, Short had a vision upon moving to Los Angeles, while some of his methods may have seemed unorthodox at the time. Short ushered in the new age of the Lakers as his team consistently went to the playoffs and finals, although the road to a ring took a little longer than expected. Without Short, though, the Lakers most likely would have never moved to L.A. and enjoyed the success of the following decades of dominance in the NBA.

Jeanie Buss

Jeanie Buss was the first female owner to win a NBA championship when she led the Lakers to their 17th Championship, tying for the most ever won by one team in the NBA. She played a huge part in acquiring LeBron James, who was a huge part of their success. Her father, Jerry Buss, originally bought the team in 1979 and left the Lakers to Jim Buss, Jeanie's brother. After the Lakers not being as successful as hoped, Jeanie took the executive role from her brother. From there on, Buss' new Lakers kept having better and better seasons, until finally winning the 2020 NBA championship in the NBA bubble with star players LeBron James and Anthony Davis.

Most Influential General Managers

Mitch Kupchak

Kupchak ran the Lakers' basketball operations department for 17 years (2000-2017), helping the team win five titles during his tenure.Kupchak's biggest and best move as general manager was trading Kwame Brown and the draft rights to Marc Gasol (48th in 2007) to the Memphis Grizzlies, among other considerations, for Pau Gasol.Kupchak also had success throughout his run, adding quality role players to his star power. In the 2000 offseason, he traded Glen Rice for Horace Grant (in a complex four-team deal) to play alongside Shaquille O'Neal. The 2000-01 Lakers nearly swept through the postseason, with a single loss to the Philadelphia 76ers in the NBA Finals. He made many other deals, but overall he did everything he could to make Kobe Bryant successul and happy.

Lou Mohs

Mohs was general manager of the Lakers from 1961-1967, when he tragically died. He was responsible for ushering the team into its new home in Los Angeles at the direction of then-owner Bob Short with only one player under contract, Elgin Baylor. He was able to acquire Wilt Chamberlin and Mohs also drafted a player by the name of Jerry West, who would go on to become a special Laker and an NBA icon. With the team he assembled he was able to go to six straight NBA finals, unfortunately losing all of them to the Boston Celtics.

Pete Newell

Pete Newell was a very well respected coach from winning many college champions. As a result, from 1972-1976, Newell became the General Manager of the Los Angeles Lakers. He was the man primarily responsible for bringing in Kareem Abdul-Jabbar from Milwaukee. In the trade, the Lakers gave up Junior Bridgeman, Brian Winters, Elmore Smith, and Dave Meyers. With the help of Magic Johnson, this pick up of Kareem Abdul Jabbar helped the Lakers win 5 championships throughout the 1980's. Unfortunately he was not the general manager when the Lakers actually won, but that is because he had to retire when his wife became very ill. Regardless, without Newell's pick up of Kareem Abdul Jabbar, the Lakers certainly wouldn't have won 5 championships

Most Influential Executives

Bill Sharman

Sharman is considered the Lakers "good luck charm." With the 1979 NBA draft looming, against his initial trepidation, he allowed Chicago Bulls GM Rod Thorn to call the coin flip to determine the order in which they were to pick. Thorn called Sharman's beloved "heads," and when it was announced "tails," the first pick in the draft belonged to the Lakers. Sharman elected to draft a player by the name of Earvin Johnson.

Jerry West

Although West became GM after the acquisitions of Abdul-Jabbar and Magic Johnson, he was the executive that brought Pat Riley back to the Lakers as head coach. He also drafted or traded for the remaining pieces that delivered the Lakers next four championships in the 1980s. West built "Showtime." West ushered in the era of the Lakers becoming the most winningest franchise over the next three decades. According to the L.A. Times, "Speaking to Investor's Business Daily, West said of his love of basketball: 'I've never worked a day in my life.'"West did not stop with just the 80s Lakers. When that run had stopped, he made sure his legacy was left with a run of five more championships when all was said and done. West worked it out to bring Shaquille O'Neal to the Lakers while drafting Kobe Bryant. That combo led to a three-peat after West wooed coach Phil Jackson to lead his team after helping the Bulls win six NBA titles.

Most Influential Coaches

Phil Jackson

Jackson coached the Los Angeles Lakers from 1999 to 2004 and again from 2005 to 2011; the team won five league titles under his leadership. Jackson's 11 NBA titles as a coach surpassed the previous record of nine set by Red Auerbach. He was a huge part in Kobe Bryant's and Shaquille O'Neal's championships.

Fred Schaus

Fred Schaus was the first head coach of the Los Angeles Lakers after their move from Minneapolis, brought in by then-owner Bob Short. He proved to be a successful coach, guiding his team to the playoffs with star player Jerry West in each of his seasons. The Lakers franchise record 69 win season that year would stand as the benchmark for success in the NBA and would not be topped for nearly a quarter of a century.

Pat Riley

Regarded as one of the greatest NBA coaches of all time, Riley has won four championships with the Los Angeles Lakers during their Showtime era in the 1980's. Riley was named NBA Coach of the Year twice with the Lakers. He was head coach of an NBA All-Star Game eight times with the Western Conference team all as head coach of the Lakers
